Let me tell you the way a home loan is usually sold.

"Your rent is dead money. When you pay a home loan EMI, you are building an asset. At the end of thirty years, you own the home outright. It is an investment."

This framing is so widespread, so culturally embedded in middle-class India, that questioning it feels almost transgressive. But the math is not kind to the narrative. Run the actual numbers and you find something quite different.

---

## The Actual Cost of a 30-Year Home Loan

Let us use a realistic example. A home priced at ₹1 crore. A buyer puts in ₹20 lakh as down payment and takes a ₹80 lakh home loan at 8.5% interest over 30 years.

Here is what happens:

- **Principal borrowed:** ₹80,00,000
- **Monthly EMI (8.5%, 30 years):** approximately ₹61,500
- **Total EMI paid over 30 years:** ₹61,500 × 360 = **₹2,21,40,000**
- **Total interest paid:** ₹2,21,40,000 − ₹80,00,000 = **₹1,41,40,000**

You borrow ₹80 lakh. You pay back ₹2.21 crore.

The interest alone is 1.76 times the principal. Over thirty years, the bank extracts more than the cost of the home itself in interest payments.

This is before property taxes, maintenance, repairs, society charges, and the opportunity cost of the down payment.

---

## The Opportunity Cost No One Talks About

The ₹20 lakh down payment is real capital. If that ₹20 lakh had been invested instead in a diversified equity index fund — let us use a conservative 12% CAGR, which is roughly what the Nifty 50 has returned over long periods — it would be worth approximately:

₹20 lakh × (1.12)^30 = **₹5.99 crore**

That is the cost of the down payment, properly accounted for.

And the EMI itself? ₹61,500 per month is capital that cannot be deployed elsewhere. If that same ₹61,500 were invested every month at 12% CAGR over 30 years — a basic SIP calculation — the corpus would be approximately:

**₹21 crore.**

I am not suggesting everyone should skip buying a home. I am suggesting that "your home is an investment" is a claim that requires scrutiny that most people never do.

---

## When Does Buying Make Sense?

A home purchase can make sense under specific conditions that are rarely true simultaneously for the average middle-class buyer.

**When you plan to stay in one city for 20+ years.** Mobility has a cost when you own. Selling a property costs 3–7% in transaction costs. If you move every 5–7 years — which is common in careers today — you are consistently paying this exit cost.

**When the rental yield is low relative to purchase price.** In Mumbai, Bengaluru, or Delhi, rental yields on residential property are typically 1.5–3%. That means a ₹1 crore home rents for ₹12,000–₹25,000 per month. This is far below the EMI on that property. The math of renting and investing the difference is often superior.

**When you are buying without significant leverage.** A home bought with a 60–70% down payment looks very different from one bought with a 20% down payment. The interest burden is dramatically lower. Most buyers do not have this option.

**When the asset appreciates significantly above inflation.** Indian residential real estate has appreciated in nominal terms in many cities. But in real (inflation-adjusted) terms, the story is more mixed. And capital gains on property are taxed, illiquid, and transaction-heavy.

---

## The Psychological Dimension

None of this means homes have no value. They have enormous non-financial value.

A home provides stability, a sense of permanence, freedom to modify your space, protection from arbitrary eviction, and a form of forced savings that, however inefficient, actually happens — unlike investment intentions that often don't.

The psychological value of owning the walls you live in is real. For many people, it is worth paying for.

But that is a lifestyle decision. It should be made honestly, with clear eyes about what it costs. Not dressed up as an investment that "beats renting."

---

## What the Bank Knows That You Don't

Banks have been running this product for decades. They understand the actuarial math perfectly. They know that a 30-year home loan is among the most profitable products in their portfolio — precisely because most borrowers stay in it for the full term, paying interest compounding against them the entire way.

The first five years of a 30-year loan are almost entirely interest. In year one, of your ₹61,500 EMI, roughly ₹56,000 is interest and ₹5,500 is principal. You are nearly a decade in before the balance shifts meaningfully.

This is not a conspiracy. It is arithmetic. But it is arithmetic that most buyers never see laid out plainly.

---

## The Bottom Line

A home loan is not an investment vehicle. It is a consumption decision with a financing cost attached.

That financing cost, over 30 years at Indian interest rates, is enormous — often exceeding the value of the asset itself.

If you want to buy a home because it gives you stability, permanence, and a place that is genuinely yours — that is a completely legitimate reason. Millions of people make that trade and are glad they did.

But buy it knowing what it actually costs. Do the math. Understand what you are trading away in financial flexibility and compounded returns.

The 30-year EMI trap is not a trap because home ownership is bad. It is a trap because people walk into it believing it is an investment, when the numbers say it is a very expensive, very long-term consumption decision.

Know the difference. Then decide.
