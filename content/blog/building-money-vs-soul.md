There is a question that most builders avoid.

Not because it is difficult to answer. But because the honest answer is often uncomfortable.

Are you building what you are building because it matters to you — because it is the thing you would be doing regardless of how much it pays — or are you building it because it is a good business opportunity?

This distinction matters more than most people acknowledge.

---

## Two Modes of Building

I have watched people build in both modes. The outputs look similar from the outside. Both produce products. Both generate revenue. Both involve the same vocabulary of strategy, execution, and growth.

But they feel completely different from the inside. And over a long enough timeline, they produce completely different outcomes.

**Building for money** is optimising a transaction. You identify an opportunity, you execute toward it efficiently, and the measure of success is financial return. This is not a criticism — it is a legitimate mode of operating. There are excellent businesses built this way, and no shame in it.

**Building for the soul** is something different. It is making something because the problem it solves genuinely bothers you, or because the experience it creates genuinely matters to you, or because the world you are trying to build is one you would actually want to live in. The financial model has to work — you need to eat — but the financial model is the constraint, not the goal.

The clearest signal of which mode you are in: what would you change about the product if money were not a consideration?

---

## Why This Matters Over a Decade

In the short run, building for money often outperforms building for the soul. The financial-first builder is more ruthless about what to cut, more efficient about what to pursue, more willing to make decisions that serve the business even when they do not serve the user.

But something happens over time.

**Motivation erodes.** Building for money works well when the money is coming. When the market turns, or growth stalls, or competition intensifies, the person building purely for financial return has no other source of fuel. The intrinsic motivation is not there. The builder starts looking for the exit.

**The product drifts.** Financial-first products are vulnerable to a specific pattern: they start well, with a genuine insight at their core, and then slowly accumulate compromises that each made sense individually but collectively corrupt the original vision. Every compromise that improves a metric but worsens the experience is a small withdrawal from a bank account of meaning. Eventually the account is empty.

**The market can tell.** Users are more perceptive than builders give them credit for. They can feel — not always consciously, but consistently — when a product is built for them versus built to extract value from them. The products that generate genuine loyalty, the ones that people defend and advocate for and return to over years, are almost always built by people who genuinely cared about the problem.

---

## The Problem with Purity

I want to be careful here, because the romanticisation of soul-first building is its own trap.

The builder who claims to be building purely for the love of it and refuses to think seriously about financial sustainability is not admirable. They are reckless. A product that cannot sustain itself financially eventually stops existing. All the soul in the world does not help the user who relies on your product if you run out of runway.

The best builders I know are not naive about money. They are completely clear-eyed about the business model, the economics, the unit economics, the path to sustainability. They think hard about pricing, margins, and growth. They know the numbers.

But the financial model is in service of something they are actually trying to build — not the other way around.

This is the distinction that matters. Not "I don't care about money" — that is just denial. But "the money is the tool, not the destination."

---

## When the Two Come Apart

The hardest moment in any building journey is when what the business needs and what you would actually want to build diverge.

This happens to almost everyone eventually. The feature that would improve the metric but would degrade the experience. The market segment that is lucrative but that you find uninteresting. The pivot that the investors are pushing for that would make the business easier but would make you feel like you are working on someone else's problem.

How you navigate this moment defines a lot about what kind of builder you are — and what kind of business you end up with.

Some people take the money path and tell themselves it is temporary. "Once we get to this milestone, we will refocus on what we actually care about." In my experience, this rarely happens. The compromise that was supposed to be temporary becomes structural. The business drifts. The founder's energy fades.

Others refuse to compromise and sometimes die on the principle. The product is pure, the vision is intact, and the business never quite figured out sustainability.

The rare builders navigate this as an ongoing, honest negotiation — constantly aware of the tension, making deliberate choices about which compromises they will accept and which they will not, and keeping the core of what they are building clear even as the edges adapt to reality.

---

## What I Have Learned

Five years of building a social audio product in India has taught me a few things about this.

The decisions I am most proud of were the ones made in favour of the experience, even when the short-term metric would have favoured something else. The times we chose a slower, better onboarding flow over the faster one. The features we did not build because they would have served the business more than they would have served the user.

The decisions I am least proud of were the ones where we optimised for growth at the cost of the thing the product was actually supposed to be. Not because growth is wrong. But because every compromise of that kind made subsequent compromises easier to justify.

The product is a living record of those decisions. Users can feel it.

---

## The Question Worth Asking

If you are building something right now — a product, a company, a body of work — it is worth asking honestly:

Is this what you would be doing if the financial outcome were guaranteed to be modest but the impact were guaranteed to be real?

There is no correct answer. Some people are honest enough to say no, and they build excellent businesses with that honesty. Some people say yes, and they take the risks that come with that.

But the people who say yes and mean it have something that cannot be faked and cannot be manufactured: a source of motivation that outlasts the market cycles, the funding winters, the competitive pressures, and the years when nothing is working.

That is worth quite a lot.

Build accordingly.
