I started running seriously about four years ago. Not for a race. Not because of a health scare. I started because I needed a discipline that had no shortcuts — a practice where the only variable that mattered was whether I showed up and put in the work.

I found that the lessons running teaches are disturbingly applicable everywhere else.

---

## Pace Is Everything

The most common mistake new runners make is going out too fast. The first kilometre feels effortless. The body is fresh, the energy is there, the enthusiasm is high. So they run at a pace that feels good.

By kilometre five, they are in trouble. By kilometre eight, they are walking. By kilometre ten, they have stopped.

Experienced runners know that the pace you start at determines everything that comes after. The goal is not to feel good now. The goal is to still be running at the end.

This maps precisely onto how people approach financial decisions, career choices, and life in general.

The person who borrows aggressively to buy a home at 28, or takes on leverage to invest in a bull market, or says yes to every opportunity because the energy is there — that person is running at a pace they cannot sustain. They feel fine in kilometre one. The pain comes later.

The disciplined operator sets a pace they can maintain for decades. Lower highs. Fewer lows. Still running when others have stopped.

---

## You Cannot Skip the Miles

There is no shortcut in endurance running. You cannot condense a year of training into a month. The body adapts slowly, through accumulated stress and recovery. You have to run the miles.

Every mile teaches the body something. Every long run builds aerobic base that cannot be faked. Every hill session teaches the legs something that flat running cannot.

The same is true for expertise. You cannot condense ten years of product experience into two. You cannot manufacture the judgment that comes from having shipped things, watched them fail, and rebuilt them. The miles have to be run.

This is why I am instinctively sceptical of people who claim shortcuts to expertise. The 30-under-30 lists that celebrate people who have been working for six years as if they have earned the same authority as someone who has been working for twenty. The course that promises to teach you what took someone a decade to learn, in eight weeks.

Some knowledge compresses. Judgment does not. Judgment requires the miles.

---

## The Days You Don't Want to Go

The most important runs are the ones you don't want to do.

The 6am run when it is cold and you stayed up late. The long run on a Sunday when you are tired from the week. The easy recovery run when your legs are heavy and every kilometre feels harder than it should.

These runs do not feel significant in the moment. They rarely produce your best performance. But they are the ones that build the habit, maintain the base, and compound into something real over years.

The same is true for the habits that build a career or a portfolio or a body of work.

Writing the essay when you don't feel like it. Making the call when you'd rather not. Doing the work when the motivation has faded and what remains is only the decision to keep going.

The people who build things that last are not the ones who had the most inspiration. They are the ones who showed up on the days when inspiration was absent and did the work anyway.

---

## Recovery Is Not Weakness

Early in my running, I treated rest days as lost days. Every day not running felt like a day falling behind.

This is wrong, and it leads to injury.

The body does not get stronger during the run. It gets stronger during recovery. The stress of the run creates the stimulus. The rest is where adaptation actually happens. If you skip recovery, you accumulate stress without the adaptation. Eventually, something breaks.

I see the same pattern in people who cannot stop working. The founder who never takes a holiday. The investor who is always in deal flow. The builder who never has a fallow period.

They mistake continuous activity for progress. But creativity, judgment, and genuine insight require rest. The brain needs time to integrate, consolidate, and surface what is important.

The best thinkers I know have deliberate fallow periods. They read without agenda. They walk without their phone. They create space for the integration that continuous input prevents.

Recovery is where the compounding happens. This is true in running and in everything else.

---

## What the Long Run Teaches

When you run long — beyond two hours, into the territory where the glycogen runs low and the body starts negotiating — you learn something about yourself that is difficult to learn any other way.

You learn what you are actually made of when the conditions stop being comfortable.

Most of life is lived in comfortable conditions. The decisions that define you happen in the moments when it is not comfortable — when the market is down and everyone is selling, when the company is struggling and the easy path is to exit, when the relationship requires work you don't feel like doing.

The long run is training for those moments. It teaches you that discomfort is survivable. That the negotiating voice in your head that says "you can stop now" is not the final word. That the only question is whether you choose to keep going.

---

## The Compounding of Consistency

Four years of running have not made me fast. I am not winning races. My times are modest.

But I can run for hours without stopping. I recover faster than I used to. I have a resting heart rate that my doctor comments on approvingly. And I have a practice — a repeatable, reliable, non-negotiable daily input — that has compounded quietly into something I value more than most of what I have built or bought.

The market does not care whether your streak is impressive. The body does not care whether you enjoyed today's run. The only thing that matters is whether the practice continues.

Consistency compounds. The only question is whether you have enough patience to let it.

That is the bottom line.
